Campus discourse through University administrators’ words

By Emily Forgash

December 8, 2023

Since Hamas’ Oct. 7 attack on Israel, top University officials and leadership have sent at least 49 emails concerning the Israel-Hamas war and ensuing campus conflicts to students, faculty, alumni, and other community affiliates.

Spectator conducted a quantitative analysis of the emails, which were obtained either from University spokespeople or University webpages, to examine Columbia’s response to the war as its effects have reverberated across campus and as administrators have faced increased scrutiny—both from within the Columbia community and nationally.

The analysis showed the frequency of specific words used by administrators, including umbrella terms such as Islamophobia, antisemitism, Israel, and Gaza. Many administrators in their emails discussed the harmful impacts of doxxing incidents in conjunction with Islamophobia.

For example, in her Oct. 31 email with the subject “Shared Humanity in Times of Conflict and Division,” Law School Dean Gillian Lester wrote, “Muslim, Arab, and other community members have spoken to me of their feelings that the experiences of Palestinians have been minimized or erased; that by expressing concern for Palestinians they risk being falsely labeled as supporting Hamas and terrorism; that they fear the pernicious practice of doxxing.” In an Oct. 27 email with the subject “Standing in Solidarity,” University President Minouche Shafik wrote, “Palestinian, Muslim, or Arab students have also been subjected to harassment and targeted by doxing, a dangerous form of intimidation.”

These emails came days after a “doxxing truck,” with a digital billboard displaying the words “Columbia’s Leading Antisemites” above the names and faces of affiliates who allegedly were a part of student groups that signed pro-Palestinian statements, first came to the Morningside campus.

While many students who appeared on the doxxing truck were Muslim, Palestinian, or Arab, there have been other instances of doxxing, including of Jewish students.

Stav, GS ’24, who asked to only be referred to by her first name due to safety concerns, spoke at an Oct. 10 Students Supporting Israel rally about Israel’s “deep understanding of the sanctity of life.” After a video of her speech was posted on TikTok, which has nearly 1 million views to date, Stav told Spectator that she received threatening phone calls and text messages from unknown numbers.

Spectator’s analysis found that, in the week after the Oct. 7 attack, administrators mentioned Israel-related words 72 times, whereas they mentioned Gaza-related words 16 times.

Mentions of Islamophobia and antisemitism in the emails reached a peak during the week of Oct. 29, and mentions of Islamophobia only outpaced mentions of antisemitism during two weeks since the conflict began.

In addition, the words “terror,” “terrorist,” and “terrorism” were used 25 times within the 49 emails. The first mention came on Oct. 8 from School of General Studies Dean Lisa Rosen-Metsch, GS ’90, in which she called Hamas’ attacks on Israel “terrorist attacks.” Shafik first used the word “terror” in an email on Oct. 18 to refer to the attacks on Israel. At the start of the war, multiple students told Spectator they were frustrated that administrators did not immediately call Hamas’ attack on Israel a “terrorist attack.”

The word “Palestine” was only used on its own, and not when referencing the group Students for Justice in Palestine, in two emails. Union Theological Seminary President Serene Jones used the word five times in an Oct. 18 email, one of which was to discuss “Palestine’s right to self-determination.” Joseph Greenwell, senior vice president for student affairs in University Life, used the word three times in a Nov. 9 email to share a “Support Space for Students with Family or Friends in Palestine or Lebanon.”

Over 1,100 University alumni signed an open letter titled “Alumni Letter to Columbia University Administration,” which stopped accepting signatures on Oct. 30, in which they demanded that the administration recognize “the insensitivity and failure to mention Palestine in prior emails and communication sent to members of the University. Moving forward, correct and revise communication.”

A University spokesperson previously declined to comment on the alumni letter.

[Read more: Alumni call for University action in support of Palestinian rights in open letter]

While many emails from University administrators and leadership have been scrutinized by community members, Barnard President Laura Rosenbury’s Oct. 26 email with the subject, “A Time for Action,” drew criticism for what some community members deemed “biased rhetoric.”

Rosenbury wrote, “I am appalled and saddened to see antisemitism and anti-Zionism spreading throughout Barnard and Columbia,” making her the only administrator to use the term “Zionism” in an email.

The email caught the attention of over 900 faculty, alumni, and students, who signed an open letter titled “Academic Freedom Under Attack at Barnard College: A Letter from Barnard College and Columbia University Communities in Response to Barnard President Laura Rosenbury’s 10/26 Email ‘A Time for Action.’”

“The president’s letter and provost’s actions, reflective of an emerging College policy articulated in other public emails and in private ones sent to individual members of the faculty and student body, expresses a clear movement away from academic freedom and freedom of speech when it comes to the question of Palestine,” the open letter reads. “It also subjects all faculty and students critical of the Zionist political project to the increased security, surveillance, and policing currently being implemented by the College.”

In a statement to Spectator, a Barnard spokesperson wrote that Barnard values “the cultivation of an inclusive, educational environment of diverse perspectives and beliefs” and that “the safety and well-being of our students is President Rosenbury’s top priority.”

On a national level, university administrators have faced criticism for their responses to the impact of the war on their campuses. Harvard University President Claudine Gay, University of Pennsylvania President Liz Magill, and Massachusetts Institute of Technology President Sally Kornbluth testified in front of the House of Representatives’ Education Committee on Tuesday at a hearing on rising antisemitism on college campuses. The three university leaders were questioned about their responses to antisemitism on their campuses as a result of the war.

During the hearing, Gay faced criticism for not strongly condemning student groups’ pro-Palestinian statements, and all three presidents were scrutinized for not speaking out enough against antisemitism.

Methodology

Data collection

We collected 49 emails sent by University administrators to various Columbia communities between Oct. 7 and Nov. 21. The set of emails were sourced either from University spokespeople or University webpages, and does not represent an exhaustive collection of all email communications sent out by University administrators within the aforementioned time period.

The data is available on this GitHub repository.

Israel-Gaza and antisemitism-Islamophobia/doxxing plot

Only emails signed off by a sole administrator were included in the dataset for the plot.

The y-axis values were calculated by taking the sum of Israel-related mentions and subtracting from it the sum of Gaza-related mentions in all emails sent by each individual administrator.

The x-axis values were calculated by taking the sum of antisemitism-related mentions and subtracting from it the sum of Islamophobia and doxxing-related mentions in all emails sent by each individual administrator.
